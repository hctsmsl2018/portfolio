Educational Game Project

AP Computer Science Applications, 5 weeks from November 2, 2020 to December 4, 2020

The objective of this project was to create an educational game from scratch or from an existing game using the Java programming language. My role in this project was to make questions about the Python programming language the game will ask the player when he or she dies in the game and create the functionality in the game that selects a question the player needs to answer and the question's respective answer choices and correct answer. Also, I described some of the code for the game in the project's documentation and explained the implementation for asking and answering the questions in the game in a video presentation of the game. In this project, I learned how some programming tasks might not look easy at first, and sometimes you need to think before you write in programming. Also, I learned about how a Java application can have its code saved in different files and how these files can be linked with each other. A challenge in this project I overcame was deciding how I will store the questions and answers into the application for the game and how I will randomly select them. I overcame this challenge by using arrays to store the strings that represent the questions and their respective answers and using the Math.random() method and mathematical operations to generate random numbers to select a random question. In this project, the teamwork skills of assigning tasks to teammates and deciding the deadlines for tasks are exemplified. This is because this project involves significant amounts of work that should be divided equally among teammates. Furthermore, the completion of certain tasks in this project is also dependant on the completion of preceding tasks, meaning that deadlines should be set for each task.

Numbers Riddle Project

AP Computer Science Applications, 1 week from September 7, 2020 to September 11, 2020

For this project, I worked with a partner to design a number riddle program in Java that does several operations on a number that always gives the result of 3. Also, the program needed to include certain test cases that are used to perform the operations to make sure that the program works as intended. My specific role in the project was to individually develop a version of the program that was to be compared with my partner's version of the same program. When my partner's program and my own were compared, we chose the better program out of the two we created to submit as our final product. In this project, I learned that it is a good practice to estimate the amount of time spent on the development of a certain part of a program and use that estimate as a goal for development, and log the estimated and actual development times for parts of the program on a milestone chart. Furthermore, I learned that the development of a program can often take longer than expected, and troubleshooting can take up a significant amount of time used for development. One of the challenges I overcame during this project to find and fix errors I made when writing the program after I finished creating it. A few minutes after I realized I made the error, I found it and fixed it, which made my program able to work normally again. Another challenge was identifying the tasks I want to list on the milestone chart and deciding what these tasks were. I was able to overcome this challenge by knowing the requirements of the program I needed to create and figure out including which of these requirements belong to the tasks I needed to accomplish this project. After my partner and I finalized our program, we presented our program to another pair of students who evaluated how well our code works and if it produces the intended results, and my partner and I evaluated that other team's code. This represents the teamwork skill of receiving input from another team so that one team can improve the product it makes.

Renewable Insulation Project

Principles of Engineering, 2 weeks from February 4, 2020 to February 19, 2020

In this project, I designed an insulation material with a team of 3 other people. The insulation material had to be made out of completely renewable and biodegradable materials, such as wood, paper, and clay. We researched each material we used to better understand certain factors affecting their usefulness, such as its environmental impact and cost-effectiveness. On our project documentation, we described each material according to our research. One of my main roles in the project was to contribute a layer of magazine paper to our insulation material. For the magazine paper, I took several old magazines and cut them to a size that corresponds with that of other parts of our insulation. I then added a clay border around the sheets of magazine paper used for the insulation, so that the magazine paper stays together. Another major contribution I made in this project was performing calculations for values that describe the insulator's effectiveness. For example, using experimental data gathered when testing the insulator, I calculated the thermal conductivity constant, U-value, and R-value for my insulator. When I showed my calculations for my group to check, I made sure to see if everyone agreed and solve disputes if necessary. Even though everyone else agreed with another teammate's work, I found an error in her work and got her and the rest of my team to correct it. One of my challenges during this project was being neat and exact in my cutting of the magazine paper. When I tried cutting the magazine paper using a paper cutter, the paper cutter sometimes displaced the paper rather than cutting through it, even though I tried to cut fewer sheets of paper at a time. Still, to preserve the exactness of my cutting of the paper, I cut shorter cuts at a time to better preserve the exactness of my cuts. Whenever the paper cutter can not cut through the paper without displacing it, I used a pocket knife to cut the paper, so I can prevent displacing it.

Mechanical Winch Project

Principles of Engineering, 2 weeks from October 14, 2019 to October 23, 2019

For this project, three other people and I worked on a physical model of a mechanical winch and a program using Netlogo of that winch, which is supposed to model that winch in terms of the behavior of the real-life winch. Also, a documentation describing the winch and program's functionality and development were required. The physical winch needs to have the ability to lift a mass of 100 grams for 30 centimeters. It also needs to be made of only VEX of FischerTech parts, and be powered by a FischerTech motor. The simulation of the winch must show how the size of the surface that the rope of the winch coils around and the voltage and amperage of the electrical power used to coil the rope of the winch affects the rate at which the cable of the winch coils and the angular speed of the surface in which the rope coils around. During this project, I mainly worked on the program that simulates the behavior of the winch, while I also worked a little bit on the physical prototype of the winch. I worked on all parts of the simulations, such as all of the variables affecting the speed at which the winch works on, and the background for the winch model in my simulation. On the project documentation, I included pictures of the code and interface for the program. I also made the project documentation's formatting neater and more consistant after all of my group members finished their parts of it.
